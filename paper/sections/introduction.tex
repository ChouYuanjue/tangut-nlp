\section{Introduction}

Historical translation at the ultra-low-resource end is difficult for a different reason than ordinary low-resource MT. The bottleneck is not only data scarcity, but also output shape: many targets are compact titles, glosses, or catalog strings rather than fluent modern sentences. Tangut is a sharp case. In the repository studied here, only 491 real Tangut-Chinese pairs are available, and many targets are short title-like strings rather than ordinary sentences \cite{sommerschield2023ancient, wang2023evahan, domingo2018modernizing}. A system can therefore sound plausible while still missing the real scholarly target, namely the correct historical title in the correct form.

This changes the paper's central question. Once a strong frontier Chinese LLM is already given dictionary glosses and a title-oriented prompt, the issue is no longer whether a local model can simply beat it head-to-head. The more useful question is what remains worth learning locally, and whether local hypotheses can still improve a controlled workflow under matched references.

We therefore organize the paper around three research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{RQ1}: How far can strong frontier prompting go as a single-model upper bound?
\item \textbf{RQ2}: If local training still matters, what kind of supervision signal helps most?
\item \textbf{RQ3}: Do frontier and local systems fail differently enough to yield a reproducible workflow gain?
\end{enumerate}

We answer these questions by comparing local dictionary-grounded prompting, a deliberately strong frontier prompt-only baseline, synthetic pseudo-parallel SFT, preference optimization on top of SFT, and inference-time selection over frontier and local candidates \cite{chen2017incorporating, sennrich2016monolingual, rafailov2023dpo}. The design is layered on purpose: first establish the strongest single-model upper bound, then identify the best open/local recipe, and only then ask whether complementarity can be turned into a workflow gain. To keep that final claim honest, we also include simple open reranking controls such as plurality vote, pairwise chrF-MBR, and a local Qwen reranker over the same candidate pool.

The resulting story is simple. Frontier prompting is the strongest single-model strategy. Within the open/local regime, structured multitask supervision is the right base, and DPO helps only after aggressive preference-gap filtering. The main positive result is not local replacement of the frontier system, but recovery of complementary conservative hypotheses through reranking. The open rerankers already provide a reproducible gain, while the stronger closed adjudicator is used only as an upper bound on recoverable complementarity. A supplementary oracle-bone stress test then checks portability without moving the paper's center: dictionary-grounded prompting remains the strongest single oracle model, but a simple open 2-way hybrid with MT-SFT still improves over either component alone.

This framing is deliberate. We do not position the paper as a scholar-in-the-loop utility study. A small expert panel would answer a different question from the one studied here and would still be too small to support strong claims about expert consensus. We therefore first narrow the model and workflow story under matched references, then reserve expert-facing validation for follow-up external evaluation.

The paper makes three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a research-question-driven case study that separates three claims often blurred together in low-resource work: the strongest single-model upper bound, the best open/local trainable recipe, and the extent to which complementary local hypotheses can be recovered in a workflow under only 491 real pairs;
\item a positive local-method result showing that structured multitask supervision is the right base, that DPO helps only after aggressive preference-gap filtering, and that local models matter because they contribute complementary conservative hypotheses rather than because they dominate the frontier model head-to-head;
\item a reference-aware evaluation and reranking analysis showing why short historical titles must not be judged primarily by generic reference-free metrics, and how different open rerankers behave on the same frontier/local candidate pool.
\end{enumerate}

Tangut remains the only benchmark here with matched real short-title translations and the only setting where the full frontier/local workflow claim is made.
