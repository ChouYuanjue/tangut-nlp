\section{Introduction}

Machine translation for historical ultra-low-resource languages is hard for two separate reasons: parallel data are scarce, and the outputs that matter to scholars are often short titles or gloss-like strings rather than fluent modern sentences. Recent work on ancient-language NLP and historical-text translation has made this setting more visible, but it has also shown that both supervision and evaluation become unstable outside modern benchmark conditions \cite{sommerschield2023ancient, wang2023evahan, domingo2018modernizing}. Tangut is a canonical example. In the current repository snapshot, only 491 real Tangut-Chinese pairs are available.

This regime also creates an obvious objection: perhaps a strong Chinese frontier LLM, given dictionary glosses and a careful title-oriented prompt, is already enough. We therefore compare five components in one frame: local dictionary-grounded prompting, a stronger proprietary frontier prompt-only baseline, synthetic pseudo-parallel SFT, preference optimization on top of SFT, and a new inference-time hybrid adjudication workflow \cite{chen2017incorporating, sennrich2016monolingual, rafailov2023dpo}. The point is to separate what prompting already buys, what local training still buys, and whether the two can be combined into a stronger scholar-facing system.

Our findings are straightforward. The stronger prompt-only objection is real: the DeepSeek-V3.2 few-shot dictionary baseline is the strongest single model we test, with 2.80/5 overall under the reference-aware judge and 12/50 exact matches. Within the open/local trainable regime, structured multitask SFT is the right base, and DPO helps only after it is moved onto that base and the preference pairs are filtered much more aggressively; the strict gap-filtered sigmoid variant reaches the highest local chrF++ in the repository (30.01). Complementarity is also not merely an oracle observation. A catalog-aware three-way adjudicator over the frontier output, UNK-SFT, and the strict gap-filtered DPO model reaches 34.87 chrF++, 3.02 overall, and 14 exact matches, while a deterministic open selector over the same pool still recovers part of that gain without any closed adjudicator. Finally, the repository exposes two robustness issues directly: several reference-free metrics mis-rank the gold reference itself, and low-gap preference data remain noisy enough to destabilize DPO.

The current draft therefore makes three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item an empirical comparison that cleanly separates the strongest single model, the strongest local trainable recipe, and the strongest hybrid workflow under only 491 real pairs;
\item a positive local-method result showing that structured multitask SFT is the right base and that strict gap-filtered DPO produces the strongest local model, while catalog-aware hybrid adjudication turns those local candidates into a better workflow and a deterministic open selector recovers part of the same gain;
\item a reference-aware evaluation, robustness, and data-hygiene analysis showing why reference-free metrics and low-gap preference data can mislead in this task.
\end{enumerate}

The rest of the paper situates the task against related work, describes the comparison families, and then keeps two claims separate: how to build the best open Tangut model, and how to combine frontier and local candidates into a stronger workflow.
