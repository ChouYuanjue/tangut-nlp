\subsection{Reference-aligned diagnostics}

Table~\ref{tab:diagnostics} summarizes the most relevant output-side diagnostics. The frontier DeepSeek baseline is already a clean single model: 12/50 exact matches, 0 contamination, 24/27 suffix-preserving titles, and a near-reference length ratio of 0.95. Within the local family, multitask SFT is the cleanest pure SFT system, and strict gap-0.4 DPO regularizes the outputs further: MT-DPO g0.4 sig restores the length ratio to 1.12 and reaches the highest local chrF++ in the repository. The catalog hybrid is cleaner still, with 14/50 exact matches, 26/27 suffix preservation, 0 contamination, and the same 0.95 length ratio as the frontier baseline. The workflow gain therefore does not come from verbose rewriting; it comes from more accurate conservative title reconstruction.

\subsection{From oracle complementarity to a real hybrid}

The oracle analysis showed that frontier and local candidates were not near-duplicates. The stronger result is that this complementarity can be operationalized. Table~\ref{tab:oracle-complementarity} traces the progression from the frontier baseline, to an earlier 2-way hybrid, to the final 3-way catalog hybrid, and then to the best-of-three oracle.

\input{tables/oracle_complementarity}

The 3-way catalog hybrid changes 13/50 frontier predictions, reaches 34.87 chrF++, 3.02 reference-aware overall, and 14 exact matches, and surpasses the frontier baseline on all three summary metrics. Across the 50 test items, it selects the frontier output 37 times, MT-DPO g0.4 sig 6 times, UNK-SFT 5 times, and synthesizes only 2 conservative repairs. Thus 48/50 outputs come from direct candidate selection, not free rewriting. The exact-match union of the three candidate sources is also 14/50, so the hybrid closes the exact oracle gap completely, although the oracle still retains headroom on chrF++ and overall score.

The new reproducibility baseline is useful for a different reason. A deterministic open selector over the same frontier/UNK-SFT/MT-DPO pool already recovers part of the workflow gain without closed adjudication: it reaches 32.62 chrF++, keeps zero contamination, matches the frontier's 12/50 exact matches, and under the local Qwen judge ties the frontier baseline at 4.20 overall while still trailing the full catalog hybrid at 4.24. If one wants the best \emph{single} model, the frontier baseline is still the right choice. The role of local training is instead to supply complementary historical-title hypotheses that a conservative adjudicator can exploit. This is visible in concrete cases where the hybrid restores \zh{五部經} instead of the frontier normalization \zh{五類經}, or fixes the frontier truncation \zh{番} to \zh{番言金剛王乘根}.

\subsection{Reference-aware judging changes the ranking}

The legacy LLM judge in the repository is reference-free, so we reran evaluation with a stricter judge that sees the Tangut input, the gold reference, and the candidate simultaneously.

\input{tables/reference_aware_eval}

Table~\ref{tab:reference-aware-eval} changes the ranking. Among single models, the frontier baseline remains the clear winner at 2.80 overall. Within the local learned family, the best overall score is shared by three multitask-base DPO variants at 2.22. But once we evaluate the actual workflow system, the catalog hybrid rises to 3.02 overall, 2.94 reference agreement, and 3.00 source faithfulness. The gains are concentrated where catalog conservatism matters most: on non-suffix titles the frontier baseline scores 2.39 overall and the hybrid 2.74, and on references of length $\le 6$ the frontier scores 2.94 and the hybrid 3.24. A local Qwen2.5-7B judge preserves the same frontier $<$ hybrid $<$ human ordering (4.20, 4.24, 4.90), reducing concern that the ranking is purely a GPT-family artifact.

\subsection{Metric alignment, robustness, and filtered DPO}

The \texttt{human\_reference} run is an important anchor. The gold reference receives perfect chrF++ and perfect reference-aware judge scores, but only moderate lexical coverage and catastrophic perplexity. Lexical coverage therefore measures overlap with dictionary-derived semantics rather than agreement with the gold title, while perplexity penalizes compact title style and historical transliterations. These metrics remain useful diagnostics, but they should not drive model selection in this task.

\input{tables/statistical_robustness}

The bootstrap results in Table~\ref{tab:statistical-robustness} clarify which claims are stable. The local chrF++ gain from multitask SFT to strict gap-0.4 sigmoid DPO remains above zero under resampling, and the catalog hybrid's chrF++ gain over the frontier baseline does as well. The judge-overall and exact-match gains are directionally positive but boundary-level on this 50-example test set. The overlap audit addresses a separate concern: there is zero exact source, target, or full-pair overlap across train/dev/test; zero exact overlap between test references and the 50k synthetic multitask targets; and zero exact overlap between the frontier few-shot exemplars and the test references. This does not prove complete immunity from leakage, but it rules out the simplest exact-copy explanation.

\input{tables/dpo_audit}

The DPO audit explains why strict filtering helps. Across all 3,867 preference pairs, the reward-chosen candidate is closer to a simple gold proxy only 71.3\% of the time, but this rises to 81.5\% for gaps $\ge 0.40$. The high-gap filter is therefore isolating cleaner labels rather than acting as a cosmetic hyperparameter. Once DPO is moved onto the multitask base and filtered at 0.4, it stops behaving like a blanket negative result and becomes the strongest local model. The hybrid workflow does not erase this point; it depends on the local candidate pool being strong enough to contribute conservative alternatives in the first place.
