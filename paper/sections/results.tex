\section{Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} summarizes the repository metrics. The central pattern is no longer a simple ``SFT good, DPO bad'' split. Zero-shot generation still fails, dictionary prompting is still strong on coverage-like diagnostics, and structured multitask SFT remains the strongest pure supervised baseline. But once DPO is rerun on the multitask base with reward-gap filtering, the story changes again: strict filtering does not just rescue DPO qualitatively, it becomes the strongest overall learned configuration in the repository.

The most important raw-metric comparison now spans \texttt{baseline3\_2\_multitask}, the looser gap-$0.2$ DPO variants, and the stricter gap-$0.4$ follow-ups. The strongest new result is the strict gap-$0.4$ sigmoid DPO variant. Tightening the reward-gap threshold from 0.2 to 0.4 cuts the preference set from 1,996 pairs to 498, but it raises learned-system chrF++ to 30.01, preserves 5 exact matches, and brings the average output length back near the reference ratio (1.12). The strict robust+weighted variant is slightly weaker on chrF++ (27.52) but is still much cleaner than its gap-$0.2$ counterpart. By contrast, the looser gap-$0.2$ sigmoid model already showed reference-aware adequacy gains, yet it overgenerated badly (length ratio 1.74), while the looser robust+weighted model preserved suffixes most aggressively but contaminated 7 outputs.

These disagreements are precisely why the paper should not rely on a single automatic metric. Raw chrF++, contamination, and the later reference-aware judge each reward different parts of the short-title problem. In the current Tangut setting, the more defensible claim is now stronger but also more specific: structured SFT is the best starting point, and high-gap multitask-base DPO can improve on it. Loose filtering favors some adequacy gains at the cost of unstable surface behavior, while strict filtering yields the best overall raw/clean trade-off.
