\section{Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} reports only representative systems. The empirical story is short: a strong frontier baseline already answers much of the single-model question, local learning helps only when it stays close to title structure, and the main reproducible gain comes from selecting among complementary candidates rather than asking one local model to replace the frontier system.

\subsection{RQ1: Is Strong Frontier Prompting Already Enough as a Single-Model Upper Bound?}

Yes. The DeepSeek frontier baseline is far stronger than the local prompt baselines, reaching 28.54 chrF++, 12/50 exact matches, and 2.80 overall under the reference-aware judge. Prompt-only is therefore not a straw baseline here. A strong frontier system is already hard to beat, so every later claim has to explain what local training still contributes after that bar is in place. The frontier configuration is also fully enumerated now: \texttt{deepseek/deepseek-v3.2} via OpenRouter, temperature 0.0, maximum 512 output tokens, and four fixed development-set exemplars. Because decoding is deterministic, sampling seeds are not relevant. We do not claim a full paid prompt-variance sweep, but the stored pilot variants in the repository support the chosen prompt direction: on the shared 5-item pilot slice, the final few-shot title-only prompt reaches 2/5 exact matches and 67.68 chrF++, versus 0/5 and 21.99 chrF++ for the strict title-only prompt.

\input{tables/local_prompt_controls}

The new local prompt controls answer two reviewer questions directly. Reusing the exact frontier-style few-shot prompt on local Qwen2.5-7B lifts chrF++ from 7.59 for the legacy local CoT prompt to 12.18 and reduces contamination from 5/50 to 3/50, but exact match stays at 1/50. Adding constrained beam search over one filtered content phrase plus one suffix constraint moves chrF++ only marginally to 12.35, again with 1/50 exact matches and slightly worse contamination at 4/50. The frontier advantage is therefore not just better wording wrapped around the same local model, and the missing constrained-decoding baseline does not explain away the single-model gap.

\subsection{RQ2: If Local Training Still Matters, What Signal Helps Most?}

The answer is not that more local training is always better. What helps is supervision that explicitly teaches the model how a short historical title is assembled. UNK-SFT already improves over prompt-only local decoding by enforcing pure Tangut inputs, and multitask SFT improves again, making structured supervision the right base. DPO is not uniformly helpful: the legacy DPO checkpoint trained on the old UNK-selected base drops to 19.39 chrF++ and 1.68 overall, so reward-based alignment is clearly not a blanket win. Once DPO is moved onto the multitask base and filtered aggressively, however, it becomes useful: MT-DPO g0.4 sig is the strongest local model at 30.01 chrF++ and 2.22 overall. The gap-0.4 robustWPO control reaches the same overall score but lower chrF++, so the main gain is still better explained by cleaner labels and the stronger multitask base than by a more elaborate loss alone.

\subsection{RQ3: Can Complementary Failures Be Turned into a Stronger Workflow?}

Yes, and this is where the paper's main positive result appears. The frontier model remains the best single predictor, but the best local models are still useful because they fail differently and often more conservatively on short titles. Table~\ref{tab:oracle-complementarity} includes not only our guarded selector, but also standard open reranking controls over the same 3-way frontier/UNK/DPO pool. Even a trivial plurality vote already improves the frontier baseline to 30.06 chrF++, 13/50 exact matches, and 2.94 overall, which shows that complementarity is visible without any learned scorer. A local Qwen reranker reaches 30.85 chrF++ and 2.82 overall. Pairwise chrF-MBR pushes overlap higher to 34.76 chrF++, yet it over-switches and hurts judged quality, dropping to 2.64 overall. The guarded heuristic is therefore not the only useful open reranker, but it is the strongest clean overlap-oriented one, reaching 32.62 chrF++ and 2.90 overall with zero contamination. The 3-way catalog-aware adjudicator still goes further to 34.87 chrF++, 3.02 overall, and 14/50 exact matches, but we treat that result only as a closed upper bound on recoverable complementarity.

The workflow result should therefore be read in two layers: open reranking over frontier and local candidates already helps reproducibly, and the closed adjudicator shows that the same pool still contains additional recoverable complementarity. Pool ablations and sensitivity checks in the repository do not change this ordering.

\subsection{Supplementary Oracle Portability Probe}

Table~\ref{tab:oracle-portability} reports a deliberately conservative second-script stress test: a 200-item held-out canonicalized OBIMD$\rightarrow$EVOBC probe. It evaluates text-side decipherment over real OBIMD reading sequences after canonicalization into the EVOBC inventory and does not reuse the Tangut-specific reranker or the reference-aware judge. Zero-shot Qwen largely refuses the surrogate input, but dictionary grounding immediately makes prompt-only transfer usable at 40/200 exact matches and 46.20 chrF++.

\input{tables/oracle_portability_probe}

The key portability signal is therefore not local single-model dominance, but survival of complementary repair behavior under a harsher interface. Reading the oracle multitask checkpoint through its \texttt{<literal>} field yields 29/200 exact matches and 40.56 chrF++, and a simple open 2-way selector already lifts the dictionary-grounded prompt from 40/200 to 44/200 exact matches while reducing contamination from 3.5\% to 0.5\%. This supports the same workflow-level claim as the main Tangut benchmark.
