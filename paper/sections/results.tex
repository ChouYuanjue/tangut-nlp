\section{Main Results}

To keep the paper centered on the research questions, Table~\ref{tab:main-results} reports only representative systems. The full ablation matrix, including zero-shot prompting, mixed and semantic SFT, gap-0.2 DPO, robust DPO, and broader workflow variants, remains in the repository and does not change the conclusions. The empirical story is short: a strong frontier baseline already answers much of the single-model question, local learning helps only when it stays close to title structure, and the main reproducible gain comes from selecting among complementary candidates rather than asking one local model to replace the frontier system.

\subsection{RQ1: Is strong frontier prompting already enough as a single-model upper bound?}

Yes for the single-model question. The DeepSeek frontier baseline is far stronger than the local prompt baselines, reaching 28.54 chrF++, 12/50 exact matches, and 2.80 overall under the reference-aware judge. The important consequence is interpretive as much as empirical: prompt-only is not a straw baseline here. A strong frontier system is already hard to beat, so every later claim has to explain what local training still contributes after that bar is in place.

\subsection{RQ2: If local training still matters, what signal helps most?}

The answer is not that more local training is always better. What helps is supervision that explicitly teaches the model how a short historical title is assembled. UNK-SFT already improves over prompt-only local decoding by enforcing pure Tangut inputs, but multitask SFT improves again, making structured supervision the right base. DPO is not uniformly helpful: the legacy DPO checkpoint trained on the old UNK-selected base drops to 19.39 chrF++ and 1.68 overall, so reward-based alignment is clearly not a blanket win. Once DPO is moved onto the multitask base and filtered aggressively, however, it becomes useful: MT-DPO g0.4 sig is the strongest local model at 30.01 chrF++ and 2.22 overall. The omitted gap-0.2 run is much worse at 20.93 chrF++, showing that label filtering, not DPO alone, drives the gain.

\subsection{RQ3: Can complementary failures be turned into a stronger workflow?}

Yes, and this is where the paper's main positive result appears. The frontier model remains the best single predictor, but the best local models are still useful because they fail differently and often more conservatively on short titles. A deterministic open selector over frontier, UNK-SFT, and MT-DPO g0.4 sig already reaches 32.62 chrF++ and 2.90 overall, so part of that complementarity is recoverable without closed adjudication. A 3-way catalog-aware adjudicator goes further to 34.87 chrF++, 3.02 overall, and 14/50 exact matches, but we treat that result as a closed upper bound on recoverable complementarity rather than as the paper's main operational claim.

Two supplemental controls sharpen this claim. First, pool ablation shows that each local candidate family contributes real but limited complementarity on its own: frontier+UNK, frontier+MT-SFT, and frontier+DPO each repair one additional exact match and raise chrF++ from 28.54 to 30.05, while the full 3-way open selector reaches 32.62 chrF++, indicating that the larger gain is distributed across local candidates rather than explained by a single pairwise fix. Second, a targeted sensitivity sweep over frontier prior, title-suffix bonus, and guarded switch margin keeps the 3-way selector between 32.33 and 33.48 chrF++ with 11--12 exact matches and zero contamination, so the open gain is not a brittle one-setting artifact.

The workflow result should therefore be read in two layers. Operationally, the open selector is the main reproducible gain. Analytically, the closed adjudicator shows that the candidate pool contains additional complementarity that a stronger reranker can exploit. The core claim is intentionally modest: frontier prompting is the single-model upper bound, MT-DPO g0.4 sig is the strongest local model, and local training matters because it supplies complementary conservative hypotheses that even an open selector can exploit.
