\section{Conclusion}

The final message of this paper is now more precise and more useful. Strong frontier prompting does not fail on Tangut short-text translation; it is the strongest single-model baseline. Within the open/local regime, however, structured multitask supervision remains the right base recipe, and aggressively filtered multitask-base DPO produces the strongest local model. More importantly, those local models are not merely weaker copies of the frontier output. When their complementary historical-title hypotheses are combined through catalog-aware adjudication, the resulting workflow becomes the strongest non-reference system overall, reaching 34.87 chrF++, 3.02 reference-aware overall, and 14/50 exact matches.

This changes the practical lesson. For ultra-low-resource historical short-text translation, the right question is not simply whether local models can replace a frontier API. It is whether structured local training can produce conservative alternative hypotheses that improve a scholar-facing workflow. In this repository, the answer is yes. The broader methodological lesson is equally important: evaluation must be anchored to reference-aware criteria, because generic reference-free metrics can badly mis-rank compact historical titles and even penalize the gold reference itself.
