Tangut short-text translation combines two difficulties: the data are extremely scarce, and the targets are often compact historical titles rather than ordinary modern sentences. In our repository snapshot, only 491 real Tangut-Chinese pairs are available, which makes the central question sharper than in ordinary low-resource MT. Once a strong frontier Chinese LLM is already equipped with dictionary glosses and a title-oriented prompt, what remains worth learning locally? We answer this with a layered case study: first establish a deliberately strong frontier prompt-only upper bound, then compare synthetic local supervised fine-tuning (SFT) recipes and filtered direct preference optimization (DPO), and finally test whether frontier and local candidates can be combined through several open rerankers and a closed catalog-aware adjudicator. The paper is intentionally framed as a controlled benchmark and workflow comparison rather than as a scholar-in-the-loop deployment study: the goal here is to narrow the most reliable model and reranking families under matched references, while expert-facing utility validation is reserved for follow-up work once the candidate workflow has stabilized. The pattern is consistent. Frontier prompting is the strongest single-model strategy. Within the open/local regime, structured multitask supervision is the right base, and DPO helps only after aggressive preference-gap filtering. Local training is still useful not because it replaces frontier prompting, but because it contributes conservative alternative titles that improve downstream selection: simple open reranking already helps, whereas generic consensus maximization can overshoot and damage title quality. A supplementary 200-item canonicalized oracle-bone stress test over EVOBC IDs shows the same directional pattern under a deliberately minimal transfer setup: dictionary-grounded prompting is the strongest single oracle model, but a simple open 2-way hybrid with MT-SFT still lifts exact match from 40/200 to 44/200, reaches 47.81 chrF++, and reduces contamination to 0.5\%. The broader lesson is to frame ultra-low-resource historical translation as workflow design under reference-aware evaluation, since lexical coverage and perplexity can mis-rank the gold reference itself.
