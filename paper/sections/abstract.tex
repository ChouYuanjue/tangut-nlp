Tangut short-text translation combines two difficulties: the data are extremely scarce, and the targets are often compact historical titles rather than ordinary modern sentences. In our repository snapshot, only 491 real Tangut-Chinese pairs are available, which makes the central question sharper than in ordinary low-resource MT. Once a strong frontier Chinese LLM is already equipped with dictionary glosses and a title-oriented prompt, what remains worth learning locally? We answer this with a layered case study: first establish a deliberately strong frontier prompt-only upper bound, then compare synthetic local supervised fine-tuning (SFT) recipes and filtered direct preference optimization (DPO), and finally test whether frontier and local candidates can be combined through an open selector or a closed catalog-aware adjudicator. The pattern is consistent. Frontier prompting is the strongest single-model strategy. Within the open/local regime, structured multitask supervision is the right base, and DPO helps only after aggressive preference-gap filtering. Local training is still useful not because it replaces frontier prompting, but because it contributes conservative alternative titles that improve downstream selection. The broader lesson is to frame ultra-low-resource historical translation as workflow design under reference-aware evaluation, since lexical coverage and perplexity can mis-rank the gold reference itself.
