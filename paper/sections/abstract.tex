Tangut translation is an extreme low-resource problem: the current repository contains only 491 real Tangut-Chinese pairs, split 400/41/50 into train/dev/test, and many targets are compact title-like Chinese strings rather than ordinary sentences. We compare local prompt-only baselines, a stronger frontier prompt-only baseline, synthetic-data supervised fine-tuning (SFT), direct preference optimization (DPO), and inference-time hybrid adjudication. DeepSeek-V3.2 with dictionary grounding and four fixed few-shot exemplars is the strongest single model, reaching 2.80/5 on a reference-aware judge with 12/50 exact matches. Within the open/local regime, structured multitask SFT is the best supervised base, and DPO helps only on that base with strict preference-gap filtering: the gap-0.4 sigmoid variant reaches 30.01 chrF++ and is the strongest local model. A catalog-aware adjudicator over the frontier output, UNK-SFT, and that DPO model yields the strongest non-reference system overall, with 34.87 chrF++, 3.02 reference-aware overall, 14/50 exact matches, and zero contamination. Bootstrap resampling confirms a robust chrF++ gain over the frontier baseline (+6.33, 95\% CI [2.10, 11.67]); judge and exact-match gains are positive but boundary-level on this 50-item test set. A local Qwen judge preserves the same frontier $<$ hybrid $<$ human ordering, and a deterministic open selector over the same pool still reaches 32.62 chrF++, showing that part of the workflow gain is reproducible without closed adjudication. Finally, lexical coverage and perplexity mis-rank the gold reference itself, so reference-aware evidence should be treated as primary and reference-free metrics as diagnostic. The practical lesson is two-level: strong frontier prompting is the best single-model upper bound, but structured local training remains useful because it supplies complementary historical-title hypotheses for a stronger scholar-facing workflow.
