\section{Discussion and Limitations}

This paper should not be read as a collection of ablations in search of the highest score. It is a question-driven case study of what changes once a strong frontier model is already available under extreme scarcity. Under only 491 real pairs, the remaining scientific question is not whether a local model can beat that frontier system outright, but what kind of local signal is still worth learning. In this repository, the answer is structural conservatism: local models trained with explicit alignment scaffolding are more willing to stay close to short historical title structure, which makes them useful complements even when they are not the best standalone systems.

This changes how the contribution should be framed. The value of local training is not that it beats a frontier API across the board. Its value is that it diversifies the candidate set with hypotheses that fail differently from frontier prompting. For scholar-facing historical translation, that is often the more realistic goal: produce a small pool of plausible, conservative titles and then choose among them with a title-aware reranker rather than a generic fluency prior. In that sense, the paper's most reproducible positive claim is the open-selector result, now strengthened by explicit pool-ablation and parameter-sensitivity controls. The stronger closed adjudicator should be read as evidence that additional complementarity exists, not as the main deployable artifact.

The scope remains narrow. The current data support Tangut short-text translation, especially title-like strings, not open-domain sentence translation. The synthetic corpus is a structural proxy rather than naturally occurring pseudo-parallel data, and the strongest gains are concentrated on short and mid-length titles. The adjudication results are therefore best read as evidence about workflow design under extreme scarcity, not as a claim that closed-model reranking solves historical translation in general.

Two limitations are central. First, the dataset is tiny, so some judge and exact-match gains remain borderline even when the direction is stable. Second, the strongest adjudicator and reference-aware judge are closed models, and we do not yet have blinded expert validation. The next steps follow directly from those limits: build an open local reranker from the released adjudication traces, validate the judge on a small expert-annotated subset, and test whether the same ``frontier upper bound + conservative local complement'' pattern transfers to other historical short-title datasets.
